The shape of a Staff answer

A Staff-level system design answer is not "a long answer." It is a directed sequence of five acts with explicit transitions. The interviewer's job is to spot the act you're in and push you forward or sideways. Your job is to keep the act clear, move when it's done, and signal tradeoffs loudly so they can probe them.

ActMinutesWhat you produceSignal you're done when
1. Frame0–5Requirements + non-goals + back-of-envelope numbersInterviewer says "ok, design it" or nods
2. High-level5–15Boxes and arrows; client → gateway → limiter → serviceInterviewer picks something to drill into
3. Deep dive (×2–3)15–35Pick 2–3 areas and go to API/algorithm/DS levelInterviewer pivots to a different concern
4. Cross-cutting35–42Failure modes, monitoring, observability, securityYou have 3+ minutes left and no more questions
5. Tradeoffs + close42–45What you'd do differently with more time"Thanks, that's it"

Why the time budget matters. The single most common failure mode in Staff loops is running out of time in act 3 with no breadth shown. The fix is to land act 1 in under 5 minutes. Most candidates spend 10–12 minutes asking clarifying questions. You have 5.

The 5-act narrative spine, applied to rate limiting

Act 1 — Frame (0–5 min)

Open with the problem class, then anchor with numbers. Talk while you write the requirement bullets on the whiteboard/screen. Use your rate-limiter.md "Summary & Interview Framing" section as the seed.

  • Problem class: "Rate limiting is admission control against a configured quota. Different from throttling (shape behavior) and load shedding (system-survival). I'll cover all three if time permits but the question is about rate limiting."
  • Concrete ask: "Design a distributed rate limiter for an API gateway handling 500K RPS across 3 regions, per-user limits enforced globally, fault-tolerant."
  • Back-of-envelope:
    • 500K RPS / 3 regions ≈ 167K RPS per region
    • Assume 1B users, 100M daily actives, 1% of users generate 50% of traffic → 1M heavy users
    • Per-key state: ~50 bytes → 50 MB state in Redis for 1M active keys (trivial)
    • Latency target for limiter: p99 < 5ms (must not be the bottleneck)
  • Non-goals (say these aloud): "Not designing the auth layer. Not designing the gateway routing. Not picking the cloud."

Source: system-design/rate-limiter.md lines 14–22 (Summary), lines 24–36 (throttling vs rate limiting vs load shedding table).

Act 2 — High-level (5–15 min)

Draw boxes. Say each arrow aloud as you draw it. Aim for 6–8 boxes max — the interviewer should see the whole thing on one screen.

   Client ──► API Gateway (anycast) ──► Limiter Service ──► Origin Service
                                          │
                                          ├─► Redis (state)
                                          ├─► Config Service (limits, rules)
                                          └─► Metrics Pipeline

Call out the three things that make this Staff-level (not SDE2):

  1. Limiter is a separate tier, not a library in the gateway. Why: independent scaling, independent deploy, can be shared across gateway fleets.
  2. State lives in Redis (or equivalent), not in-process. Why: in-process means per-pod state, so N pods each see 1/N traffic and the effective limit is N× configured.
  3. Config is hot-reloadable. Why: rate limits change faster than deploys. Ops needs to lower a limit at 2am without a release.

Source: system-design/rate-limiter.md lines 168–227 (Architecture & Deployment).

Act 3 — Deep dive (15–35 min, pick 2–3)

The interviewer's signal: "Tell me more about X" or "How does the limiter actually make the decision?" That's the cue to pick one of these three deep dives. You pick 2–3, not all — the trade is depth × 2 vs breadth × 4.

Deep dive A: Algorithm choice (15 min)

Open with the comparison table from your deep-dive article, then commit to one and justify.

AlgorithmBurst toleranceMemoryPrecisionWhen I'd pick it
Token bucketHigh (up to capacity)O(1)Smooth, lazy refillDefault for public APIs (AWS, Stripe)
Sliding window counterBoundedO(1)ApproximateHigh-throughput prod, no burst surprise (Cloudflare)
Fixed window2× burst at seamO(1)CounterTrivial impl, low-stakes APIs
Sliding window logBoundedO(N)ExactLow-throughput, precision critical

Verbalize the choice: "I'd pick token bucket with sliding window counter as a fallback for noisy-neighbor tenants. Token bucket gives burst tolerance — important for legitimate clients that batch — and O(1) state. Sliding window counter fixes the 2× burst problem of fixed window. The combination is what Cloudflare and Stripe ship."

Walk the math once. Capacity = burst budget. Refill rate = sustained rate. Example: capacity 100, refill 10/sec → 100 burst, 10 RPS sustained.

Source: system-design/rate-limiting-algorithms-deep-dive.md lines 1–120 (algorithm walkthroughs), your main article lines 38–80 (comparison table).

Deep dive B: Distributed correctness (10 min)

The Staff question that breaks SDE2 candidates: "If you have 100 limiter pods and 1 Redis, what happens when Redis is slow or down?"

  • Happy path: Lua script in Redis does the read-modify-write atomically. p99 ~1–3ms.
  • Redis slow: set a local fallback (e.g. token bucket in each pod) with a stricter limit. Trade: 100 pods × 1% of traffic each = strict local cap. Better than failing open and letting the origin drown.
  • Redis down: fail open or fail closed. Verbalize the trade: "Fail open means a brief outage but no false rejections. Fail closed means correct limits but a self-inflicted DoS. For this product I'd fail open at the limiter and lean on the origin's own load shedding — the limiter is defense in depth, not the only line."
  • Clock skew: don't rely on wall-clock tokens. Use Redis's monotonic TIME command or a centralized counter. Server clocks drift; tokens must be computed against a single source of time.

Source: system-design/rate-limiter.md lines 168–227 (distributed considerations), lines 228–280 (failure modes).

Deep dive C: Rule & key design (10 min)

Often missed. The hard part isn't the algorithm — it's what to limit and how to identify the client.

  • Key hierarchy: composite key {tenant_id}:{api_key}:{route}:{client_ip}. Different limits per route. Free tier gets 10 RPS, paid gets 1000 RPS, partner API gets 10K RPS — all on the same limiter.
  • Header-based overrides: X-Internal-Request: true from internal services → skip limiter. Documented. Staff move: call out the security implication — that header is forgeable from outside if your ingress doesn't strip it. Mention mitigation: only honor the header when the request comes from a known internal mesh.
  • Tenant fairness: a single big tenant shouldn't be able to evict other tenants' capacity. Use weighted fair queuing or per-tenant buckets at a higher tier.

Source: system-design/rate-limiter.md lines 100–167 (keying, rule engine).

Act 4 — Cross-cutting (35–42 min)

Hit these in order, terse but explicit. The interviewer wants to see you think about the system, not just the limiter.

  • Observability: per-decision latency (allow/deny separately), per-rule hit rate, top-N denied clients, false-positive rate (sampled against origin's success metric). Staff move: "I would A/B the limiter in shadow mode for the first week — log decisions but don't act on them — and compare against origin health metrics before flipping the switch."
  • Security: limiter is itself a target. An attacker who can poison the limiter can lock all users out (DoS) or unlock themselves (bypass). AuthN on the limiter control plane, mTLS for limiter→Redis, audit log of rule changes.
  • Multi-region: regional Redis for low latency, async replication of rules to regional clusters, accept eventual consistency of counters within a few seconds (most rate limits tolerate it).
  • Cost: at 500K RPS, every ms of Redis time is money. Lua script < 1ms vs round-trip > 5ms. Worth the complexity.

Source: system-design/rate-limiter.md lines 281–317 (operational concerns).

Act 5 — Tradeoffs + close (42–45 min)

The "what would you do with more time" answer signals Staff-level self-awareness. Two or three concrete tradeoffs, not a vague "I would test more."

  • "I'd move the limiter decision to the edge (Cloudflare Workers / Lambda@Edge) for the first 80% of traffic that's geographically static, keeping the central limiter for partner APIs and complex rule sets. Tradeoff: edge limits can't easily reach a shared Redis, so they're per-region and you accept some over-limit."
  • "I'd add a feedback loop from the origin's p99 back into the limiter. If origin p99 crosses 500ms, the limiter tightens automatically. This makes the limiter and load shedder cooperate instead of being independent."
  • "I'd consider moving from token bucket to hierarchical token bucket (HTB) if we add service-to-service limits, where each upstream service has its own sub-budget."

Close with the time check: "I'm at about 43 minutes, want me to go deeper on any of these?" That hands control back cleanly and signals you know how to manage the clock.

Drive it: 45-min cold run

This is the practice. You do not read the next section. You set a 45-minute timer, open a blank doc or whiteboard, and drive the question cold. The narrative spine above is your reference — you can glance at it for the act transitions, but the words are yours.

After you finish, the self-critique rubric below is what you grade yourself against.

Drill schedule (from your MISSION.md plan, week 1):
  • Run 1 (this week): expect to overrun into act 4 or stall in act 3. Goal is to find the gaps.
  • Run 2 (48 hrs later): smoother, hits all 5 acts, may run out of time on act 3 deep dives.
  • Run 3 (end of week 1): clean, time-budgeted, with one strong tradeoff in act 5.
